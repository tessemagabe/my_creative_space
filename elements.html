2. Comprehensive Summary of the Incident

In 2017, Equifax, a central credit reporting firm, had a severe data violation that compromised the sensitive individual information of nearly 147 million people. The cause of the violation was taking advantage of a vulnerability in the Apache Struts system, which Equifax was unable to resolve in time. This event highlighted crucial lapses in cybersecurity performance and had far-reaching outcomes for impacted individuals and the agency.

Description of Breached Data

The violation includes addresses, names, birth dates, Social Security numbers, and driver's license numbers. Additionally, credit card numbers for nearly 200,000 people and dispute documents carrying personal information for approximately 180,000 individuals were captured.

Timeline of Prime Events

  • Mar 2017: Apache Struts weakness (CVE-2017-5638) was exposed, calling for swift action.
  • May 2017: Hackers exploited the unresolved weakness of Equifax's framework and gained access.
  • July 2017: Equifax uncovered the violation but delayed public disclosure.
  • Sept 2017: Equifax publicly disclosed the breach.
  • Equifax offered free ongoing credit monitoring services to those affected.

Participants in the Violation

Equifax's IT and security team failed to manage susceptibility and respond effectively to the breach. Externally, hackers exploited the vulnerability, and later investigations linked the attack to elements of China's People's Liberation Army.

Ramifications of the Violation

The breach made Equifax a prime target for identity theft and led to significant consumer distrust, causing a steep decline in stock market prices, regulatory scrutiny, and financial penalties. Equifax ultimately agreed to a $425 million settlement for affected individuals.

Solutions Implemented

Following the breach, Equifax reinforced its cybersecurity strategy by tightening patch management policies, enhancing network segmentation, and improving monitoring and response capabilities.

3.Major Findings from the Incident

Vulnerabilities Contributing to the Breach

  • Delay in fixing the Apache Struts vulnerability.
  • Weak network isolation allows lateral traversal by hackers through the framework.
  • The encryption certificate termination date passed without proper action, obstructing breach identification.

Indicators of Compromise (IOCs)

  • Illegal access to delicate databases.
  • Encrypted data extraction over a long period.

Recommendations

  • Implement Automated Software Management: Ensure timely updates of software and systems to prevent risks.
  • Enhance Network Partitioning: Hinder lateral movement within frameworks to minimize the effect of breaches.
  • Strengthen Monitoring and Identification: Implement enhanced threat identification tools and keep updating encryption certificates.

Conclusion

The 2017 Equifax information violation serves as a crucial case study for cybersecurity experts, highlighting the significance of preemptive exposure management, stringent network security, and opportune incident response. Analyzing this event offers valuable lessons in safeguarding against similar breaches and preventing the release of delicate information.

Incident Report: Target Data Breach of 2013

Based on findings from the Committee on Commerce, Science, and Transportation(2014).

1. Executive Summary

2. Detailed Summary of the Incident

Cybercrime Marketplace: Selling Stolen Target Cards

The Target data violation, which is considered one of the most notable cybersecurity incidents in retail history, started on November 27, 2013, and took place for several weeks after attackers achieved access to the company’s system through a refrigeration contractor, named Fazio Mechanical. Fazio's network was infected with Citadel (banking Trojan) malware, which is predominantly utilized for stealing delicate data, namely online banking credentials. Hence, the attackers using the phishing attack gathered Fazio’s login credentials from the company’s portal, which gave them access to maneuver the company network. This breach jeopardized nearly 40 million Cr/Dr cards and the personally identifiable information (PII) of about 70 million guests.

The attackers launched their main attack by installing malware on Target’s point-of-sale (POS) systems, utilizing a "RAM-scraping" Trojan (Trojan.OSRAM), allowing them to collect unencrypted Cr card data as guests buy items. The malware, recognized as "BlackPOS" or "Kaptoxa," efficiently skimmed data from the memory of the POS computers and stored these data for eventual retrieval, utilizing FTP servers to move the snatched data step by step. The breach was uncovered on December 13, 2013, when Target officials noticed skeptical activity and informed the U.S. Justice Department. By December 15, the breach was mitigated, with the help of a third-party forensic group.

The Timeline

  • Nov 27 to Dec 15, 2013: The breach occurs during the hectic holiday spending season. Intruders penetrated Target's network by snatching credentials from a vendor, pretending to be an HVAC contractor. They mounted malicious software on Target's point-of-sale (POS) infrastructure, allowing them to acquire credit card info from millions of transactions.
  • Nov 27, 2013: The intruders began operation, aiming at POS systems at the entire Target's U.S. outlets.
  • Nov 30, 2013: Target's cyber protection, “powered by FireEye”, identified doubtful activity and alerted issues. However, the alerts were not taken seriously by Target's security team.
  • Dec 12, 2013: Target was informed of the skeptical activity by the DOJ. This triggered Target to examine further.
  • Dec 14, 2013: Target employed a forensic team to examine the intrusion and start deleting malicious software from the systems.
  • Dec 18, 2013: Security analyst Brian Krebs openly reported the violation on his blog, drawing widespread attention.
  • Dec 19, 2013: Target formally confirmed the violation, disclosing that nearly 40 million Cr/Dr card accounts were breached. Later, it was revealed that the personal information, i.e. names, addresses, and phone numbers, of an extra 70 million guests reported exposed.

Essential Details

The hackers manipulated vulnerable security practices through the third-party vendor to achieve access to Target's system. Once accessed, they proceeded to more susceptible areas, such as POS systems. The malware configured was capable of capturing payment card information, which was then extracted to remote servers.

Key Findings

  • • Nearly 40 million Cr/Dr card records and personal data of 70 million guests were compromised.
  • • The violation highlighted the necessity of protecting third-party gains to corporate systems.
  • • The event underscored the necessity for robust supervision, swift reaction to alerts, and enhanced network segmentation.

Response and Resources Involved

Target's actions involved a joint effort with various entities:

  • • Target enlisted cybersecurity organizations to investigate the violation and protect its impact.
  • • The U.S. Secret Service and the Department of Justice partnered in the investigation.
  • • Target extends free credit tracking and identity theft prevention services to the impacted customers. Target also instituted significant safety upgrades, such as improved “encryption and tokenization” of payment card details.

The violation had wide-ranging consequences, such as financial losses, image and credibility-related loss, and the stepping down of Target's CEO. It underscored the crucial need for resilient cybersecurity actions and vendor monitoring practices.
